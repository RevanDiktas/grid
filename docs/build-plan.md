# GridScout — Build Plan (0 → 10)

**What this is:** a step-by-step build guide to take GridScout from nothing to a sellable product. One detailed paragraph per step. Written to be handed to **Claude Code** tonight and worked through in order. Steps 0–2 are context and setup, 3–11 are the actual build, 12–16 are turning it into a business. Read it top to bottom once, then start at Step 0.

**Product in one line:** GridScout is the intelligence layer on top of the Netherlands' (and later the EU's) grid-capacity data — it tells anyone trying to plug in a large electricity load or battery *where there's room on the grid, how fast they can connect, and how to unlock more.*

**Suggested stack (lean, solo-friendly, Claude-Code-friendly):** Python (data/ETL + scoring), PostgreSQL + **PostGIS** (geospatial database), Next.js/React + MapLibre GL (map UI), FastAPI (the API layer), a templating engine (e.g., WeasyPrint or Playwright-to-PDF) for reports, and one small cloud project (a managed Postgres + a container host). Keep everything in one monorepo so Claude Code can navigate it.

> ⚠️ **Data caveat that is also your product thesis:** the grid operators publish capacity data as *indicative only* — they explicitly warn you shouldn't make investment decisions on the raw maps alone. That gap (raw indication → trustworthy decision) is exactly what GridScout fills with timelines, confidence scoring, and synthesis. So the caveat is the opportunity.

## Step 0 — Nail what you're building and for whom (before any code)

GridScout is a B2B data-and-software product that answers one high-value question — *"where can I connect a large electricity load or battery in the Netherlands, and how long will it take?"* — for a specific first buyer: **battery (BESS) and large-load developers, plus the advisors who serve them.** Do not try to build "the operating system for the grid" tonight; build the narrow wedge that one person can ship: a Netherlands-only capacity map plus an on-demand **site feasibility report**. Everything downstream (more countries, the "unlock hidden capacity" physics layer, DSO contracts) is a later expansion of this same core. Write this scope down as the first line of your README so every build decision tonight is judged against it: *does this get a Dutch BESS/large-load developer to a trustworthy connect/where/when answer faster than they can get it today?* If yes, build it; if no, defer it.

## Step 1 — Understand exactly why they'll pay and how it slots into their work

A developer siting a battery or data center is making a decision worth millions to hundreds of millions of euros, and today they answer "can I connect here?" by hiring consultants and spending weeks or months emailing grid operators — with the answer coming back as an *indicative* map they're explicitly told not to rely on. GridScout replaces that with a fast, structured, decision-grade answer, so what they're actually buying is **time and de-risking**: shaving weeks off site screening and avoiding a catastrophic mis-site. It integrates into their workflow at the earliest and most expensive decision point — *site selection and go/no-go* — before they spend on land, permits, or grid applications; the deliverable (a feasibility report or a map filter) drops straight into their existing investment memos, board decks, and lender diligence packs. Because that decision recurs on every project and because lenders/investors increasingly demand proof of connectability, the product becomes a repeated purchase and, over time, a standard input to how deals get financed. Keep this paragraph pinned next to your scope statement — every feature you build should make the customer's *go/no-go decision* faster or more trustworthy, or it's a distraction.

## Step 2 — Set up the project and environment tonight

Create one Git monorepo with clear folders — `/ingestion` (Python ETL jobs), `/db` (schema + migrations), `/api` (FastAPI service), `/web` (Next.js app), `/reports` (report generator), and `/docs` (this plan + a running data-source registry) — and open it in Claude Code so it can see the whole thing at once. Stand up a local PostgreSQL with the PostGIS extension (via Docker is easiest) and a Python virtual environment with the core libraries you'll need immediately: `requests`, `pandas`, `geopandas`, `shapely`, `sqlalchemy`, `psycopg2`, plus `entsoe-py` for the EU transmission API. Add a `.env` for secrets/tokens and a `Makefile` (or simple scripts) so common tasks — `ingest`, `migrate`, `serve`, `build-report` — are one command each, which makes iterating with Claude Code fast. Commit a skeleton README containing the scope statement from Step 0, the buyer statement from Step 1, and an empty data-source table you'll fill in Step 3; this gives Claude Code the context to make good autonomous decisions. Tonight's win condition is simply: repo runs, database is up, and you can execute an empty `ingest` command end-to-end.

## Step 3 — Acquire the data (this is the heart of the product)

The whole business rests on turning the messy, scattered, *free* Dutch grid-capacity data into one clean dataset, and the good news is that recent EU/NL transparency rules have forced most of the raw material into the open. Your primary source is the **national capacity map (Capaciteitskaart)** from Netbeheer Nederland, which shows current available capacity for both offtake (*afname*) and feed-in (*invoeding*) and — critically — is also published as a **downloadable Excel at postcode-6 granularity**, giving you structured rows to load directly. Your second, richer source is the **VIVET capacity map**, hosted by the Kadaster on the national geodata platform **PDOK**, which gives available capacity at *substation* level for both offtake and feed-in and lets you look **3, 5, and 10 years forward** (updated quarterly) — this forward view is what powers your "timeline" feature and is hard for anyone to replicate casually. Layer on the individual **DSO open data** (Liander, Enexis, Stedin, and the smaller operators Coteq, Rendo, Westland Infra) for grid topology, substation locations, waiting-list figures per station, and transport forecasts — much of it under CC BY 4.0 via `data.overheid.nl`, PDOK, and the operators' ArcGIS portals — and pull **transmission-level data from the ENTSO-E Transparency Platform API** (free token, REST/XML) for the high-voltage picture. Finally, add **GOPACS** congestion/redispatch data for the congestion-risk signal that BESS developers care about, and register **Capacitypedia** (the pan-EU capacity index) as your roadmap source for later country expansion. Where data isn't openly published, the shared **Partners in Energie** portal is the official request channel to all Dutch DSOs — start those requests early because they seed both data and the DSO relationships you'll need later.

## Step 4 — Build the ingestion / ETL pipeline

Write one small, idempotent Python job per source that fetches the raw data, parses it (Excel/CSV via pandas, geospatial via geopandas, ENTSO-E XML via `entsoe-py`), normalizes it into your common schema, and upserts it into PostGIS — and schedule them to match each source's update cadence (Capaciteitskaart ~monthly, VIVET quarterly, ENTSO-E on demand). Make every job log what it pulled, when, and any parsing failures, and store a raw snapshot of each fetch so you can reprocess history and, later, measure how capacity at a location changed over time (that history is a moat ingredient). This is exactly the kind of repetitive, well-specified work Claude Code excels at, so build one job together carefully (start with the Capaciteitskaart Excel — it's the simplest structured win), get the pattern right, then let Claude Code replicate the pattern for each additional source. The output of this step is a database that, for any postcode or substation in the Netherlands, holds current and forward capacity for offtake and feed-in, waiting-list pressure, and congestion status — refreshed automatically.

## Step 5 — Design the data model so the "answer" is easy to compute

Model the domain around the questions customers ask, not around how each source happens to be shaped: core tables for `substations` (with geometry, voltage level, operator), `areas` (postcode-6 polygons and DSO service regions), `capacity_observations` (area/substation, timestamp, offtake vs feed-in MW available, forward-year horizon, source, confidence), `waiting_lists` (requests and requested MW per station), and `congestion_events` (from GOPACS/market data). Use PostGIS geometry columns and spatial indexes everywhere, because nearly every query is "what's true at or near this point/parcel," and store the *source and as-of date* on every row so you can show provenance and freshness (which is central to being trusted). Keep a clean separation between **raw ingested facts** and **derived intelligence** (the scored answer from Step 7) so you can recompute the intelligence layer without re-fetching, and so you can A/B different scoring logic later. Have Claude Code generate the migrations and a seed of a few real Dutch locations (e.g., a congested Amsterdam postcode and an open Groningen one) so you can eyeball correctness immediately.

## Step 6 — Build the capacity map (the visible product)

Join your capacity data to geography and render it as an interactive, traffic-light map of the Netherlands — green where there's headroom, red where it's jammed, with the offtake/feed-in/forward-year toggles that mirror how the official maps and VIVET already split the data. Use MapLibre GL with vector tiles served from PostGIS (or pre-baked tiles for speed), and make the core interaction dead simple: click a location or draw an area, and a side panel opens with the raw numbers you have for that spot. At this stage the map is still just a cleaner, unified view of public data — that's fine; it's your demo surface and the skeleton the intelligence layer hangs on. Resist over-polishing the visuals tonight; correctness and the click-to-panel flow matter far more than styling, and Claude Code can iterate the UI quickly once the data flow works end to end.

## Step 7 — Build the intelligence / scoring layer (raw data → a decision-grade answer)

This is where GridScout stops being a prettier public map and becomes something worth paying for: for any location, combine the signals you hold into a single structured answer — *estimated available capacity (MW)*, *realistic connection timeline*, *congestion risk*, *flexible-connection potential*, and a *confidence score* — rather than dumping raw numbers on the user. Compute the timeline by combining VIVET's forward-year capacity, the station's waiting-list depth, and known grid-reinforcement schedules; compute congestion risk from GOPACS activity and the capacity trend; and attach confidence based on data freshness and source agreement. Use an LLM for the genuinely hard parsing and synthesis jobs — extracting structured facts from PDFs, reconciling conflicting sources, and generating the plain-language explanation of *why* a site scores the way it does — but keep the core numeric logic as transparent, testable heuristics so the output is defensible to an engineer at a battery developer. The deliverable of this step is a single internal function, `assess(location) → structured verdict`, that everything else (map panel, report, API) calls; get this right and the rest is presentation.

## Step 8 — Build the feasibility report generator (your first sellable artifact)

Turn the `assess()` output into a clean, professional, branded report (HTML → PDF via WeasyPrint or Playwright) that a developer can drop straight into an investment memo or lender pack — this is the concrete thing people will pay for before they'll pay for a subscription. The report should lead with the verdict (connectable capacity, timeline, risk, confidence), then show the supporting evidence (the capacity map excerpt, the waiting-list situation, congestion signals, and the options to connect sooner such as flexible connections or energy-hub participation), and always cite sources and as-of dates so it reads as trustworthy rather than a black box. Templatize it so generating a report for a new location is one function call, because your early sales motion is literally "give me a location, I'll send you a report," and you want that to take seconds. Have Claude Code build the template and a `build-report <location>` command; test it on three real candidate sites so you have instant demos for Step 12.

## Step 9 — Wrap it in a minimal web app (map + search + report + login)

Assemble the pieces into a simple product: a landing/search entry ("enter an address or draw an area"), the interactive map from Step 6, the `assess()` panel from Step 7, and a "generate report" button wired to Step 8, behind lightweight authentication so you can control access and track usage. Don't build billing, teams, or fancy account management yet — a basic login and a way to gate report generation is enough to run paid pilots manually. Keep the whole thing deployable as one small app so you can put it in front of a design partner within days, not weeks; the goal is a link you can send, not a polished SaaS. Claude Code can scaffold the Next.js app, wire the API calls, and handle auth quickly once the data and `assess()` function exist.

## Step 10 — Expose an API / data feed (how it embeds in their business)

Offer the same `assess()` capability as a simple authenticated API endpoint (location in → structured verdict out) plus a bulk/area query, because your most valuable, stickiest integration is when a customer wires GridScout directly into *their* siting tools, spreadsheets, and diligence workflows so it becomes part of how they work every day. This is also your highest-margin, most defensible revenue line at scale and the thing that turns a one-off report buyer into infrastructure they can't rip out. Keep the API contract clean and versioned, document it well (Claude Code can generate the OpenAPI spec and docs), and rate-limit by plan. You don't need this live tonight, but design `assess()` in Step 7 as a clean callable from the start so exposing it as an API later is trivial rather than a rewrite.

## Step 11 — Validate accuracy and start the data flywheel

Before you lean hard on sales, sanity-check your verdicts against reality: pick a set of known cases (places you or a friendly developer know the true connection situation for) and compare GridScout's output, then tighten the scoring until it's trustworthy — because your entire moat depends on being *the most accurate map*, and one confidently-wrong report destroys trust. Then instrument the flywheel: every report you produce and, crucially, every *actual outcome you later learn* (did that site connect, at what capacity, on what timeline?) gets logged against your prediction, so your accuracy compounds with usage in a way no new entrant can shortcut. Make capturing these outcomes a deliberate habit — ask design partners what actually happened, store it — because this feedback loop, more than any code, is what makes GridScout eventually unbeatable. Have Claude Code build a small internal "prediction vs outcome" logging table and dashboard now, while it's cheap, so the habit and the data start from day one.

## Step 12 — Get design partners and first paying customers

With three real feasibility reports in hand, go founder-led to ~10–15 Dutch **BESS/battery developers** first (they move fast, are software-native, and urgently need connect-and-earn siting), plus a couple of data-center developers and site-selection advisors, and use the reports as both demo and product. Aim to convert one or two into a **paid pilot or letter of intent** — price on the value of de-risking a large decision, not on your costs — and treat every conversation as product research, feeding what they actually act on back into the scoring and report. These first logos do triple duty: revenue, the outcome data that powers Step 11's flywheel, and the credibility that makes fundraising easy. Don't wait for the product to be "done" — the report-over-email motion works before the polished app exists, so start selling the moment Step 8 produces trustworthy reports.

## Step 13 — Put in pricing and packaging

Implement the money ladder once you have willing buyers: transactional **feasibility reports** (roughly €750–€2,500 each) to get people in the door and prove value, **subscriptions** (~€300–€500/mo starter, ~€1,000–€3,000/mo pro) so revenue recurs, and later **API/data licensing** and **enterprise/DSO deals** (tens of thousands to €100k+/yr) as the relationship deepens. Start by charging manually (invoice per report, per pilot) — you do not need automated billing to take money — and only add self-serve billing once volume justifies it. Anchor every price against the customer's alternative (expensive consultants and the cost of a mis-sited project), which makes these numbers look cheap, and be willing to trade early discounts for outcome data and a public reference. Keep packaging simple: one report SKU, one subscription with two tiers, and "call us" for API/enterprise.

## Step 14 — Open a grid-operator (DSO) relationship

In parallel with selling to developers, start one conversation with a Dutch DSO — Alliander/Liander is a natural first target given their open-data and open-source posture — positioning GridScout not as a threat but as a partner that improves visibility and reduces the flood of low-quality connection inquiries they field. A DSO that validates your model turns GridScout into the **neutral, trusted referee** everyone defaults to, which is the deepest moat in this category and very hard for a competitor to replicate. This is a slow relationship, so start it early even though it won't pay off for months; use the Partners in Energie channel and any warm intros you can find. Even informal validation ("your numbers look right") is worth pursuing, because it's the trust signal that later unlocks both enterprise DSO contracts and investor confidence.

## Step 15 — Line up non-dilutive money and prep the raise

Because you're solo, use the founder cheat code: **non-dilutive grants** (EIC Accelerator, Invest-NL, the WBSO R&D tax credit, and Climate-Fund/InvestAI-adjacent NL/EU programs) that fund the build without diluting you and simultaneously validate the company. In parallel, package the raise narrative — *"GridCARE for Europe: the AI-era power bottleneck on the continent that's furthest behind"* — backed by your working product, your first paying customer or LOI, and your started DSO relationship, which together convert "solo founder with a thesis" into "founder with traction in the hottest venture theme." Target European climate/energy VCs and energy-savvy generalists, and remember the single biggest fundability lever is one name-brand pilot, so sequence pilots before the raise. Get Claude Code (or a normal doc) to help you turn the two prior deliverables (the founding thesis and pitch) into a tight data room and one-pager.

## Step 16 — Climb from "map" to "moat" and from NL to the EU

Once the Netherlands wedge is working and funded, make the two moves that turn a good product into a category-defining one: **add the physics/flexibility layer** (modeling *latent* capacity that standard planning misses, and quantifying flexible-connection and energy-hub options) — the deep, hard-to-copy technology that likely warrants adding a power-systems co-founder or hire — and **expand geography** by pointing the same machine at the next market (Germany or the Nordics), using Capacitypedia and each country's now-mandated hosting-capacity data as your raw material. Do it one country at a time, grabbing each before a US player like GridCARE or an incumbent like Aurora wakes up to Europe, and deepen the referee moat by making GridScout reports a standard diligence input for lenders and infrastructure funds. The end state is the default capacity-decision layer for European electrification — a data monopoly built on a flywheel that started with a single Dutch feasibility report you generated tonight. Keep the scope discipline from Step 0 the whole way: win one thing completely before adding the next.
